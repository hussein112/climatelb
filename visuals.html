Chapter One · Water Scarcity

The River That
Runs Dry

Lebanon was once called the "water tower of the Middle East," fed by the snowmelt of the Mount Lebanon range. Today, the Litani River — the country's largest — delivers a fraction of what it once did. Groundwater aquifers are being depleted faster than they can recharge.[1]

Per capita freshwater availability has collapsed by nearly 40% since the 1960s, driven by erratic rainfall, population pressure, and decades of mismanagement compounded by climate breakdown.[2]

"By 2040, Lebanon may face absolute water scarcity — less than 500 m³ per person per year."[3]

Chapter Two · Wildfires

The Cedar
Forests Burn

Lebanon's iconic cedar forests — ancient symbols of national identity, featured on its flag — are disappearing in smoke. Prolonged droughts, record heat, and strong easterly winds have turned Lebanon's slopes into tinderboxes.[4]

The catastrophic October 2019 wildfire season burned over 9,500 hectares in a single week. In 2021, fires raged across the north during an unprecedented heatwave. Wildfire seasons are now longer, hotter, and more frequent.[5]

"Each decade, Lebanon loses more forest cover than the previous — a cycle with no natural end in sight."[4]

Chapter Three · Rising Seas

Beirut's
Sinking Shore

Lebanon's 225 km Mediterranean coastline hosts 75% of its population.[6] Beirut's Corniche — its beloved seafront promenade — already floods during winter storms that were once considered ordinary. By 2050, rising seas could submerge parts of the Akkar coast and displace tens of thousands.[7]

The Mediterranean Sea is warming faster than the global average, causing more intense storms, stronger surges, and accelerated coastal erosion. Lebanon's coastal infrastructure was not built for this world.[8]

"The Mediterranean is rising ~3.7 mm per year — 2× the rate of the 1990s."[8]

Chapter Four · Extreme Heat

Summer Without
End

Lebanese summers are becoming unbearable. In 2023, Beirut recorded its hottest September in 170 years of records.[9] The interior valleys — Bekaa and Beqaa — regularly exceed 45°C. What were once rare heatwaves lasting 2–3 days now stretch into weeks.

The urban heat island effect in Beirut — already struggling under power cuts that disable air conditioning — is intensifying. The elderly, children, and outdoor workers face life-threatening conditions with no safety net.[10]

"Lebanon experienced 23 days above 40°C in summer 2023 — compared to 4–5 days annually in the 1980s."[9]
